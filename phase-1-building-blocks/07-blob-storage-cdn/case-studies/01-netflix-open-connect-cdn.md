# Case Study: Netflix Open Connect — A Purpose-Built CDN Inside ISPs

> How Netflix shipped its own video-cache appliances into ISP networks worldwide, turning peering economics on their head and serving the majority of its bytes from inside the ISP's own datacenter.

## Context

By 2012 video was the majority of internet traffic and Netflix alone accounted for over a third of North-American peak bandwidth. Paying commercial CDNs (Akamai, Limelight, Level3) for petabytes of egress per day was expensive *and* slow — ISP-to-CDN peering was congested at every interconnect. Netflix's response was **Open Connect**: a custom CDN built around a single appliance design (the **Open Connect Appliance**, OCA) that Netflix ships to ISPs **for free**, in exchange for the ISP racking and powering it. Netflix Tech Blog, "Open Connect Everywhere" (2016) and the Open Connect deployment guide on `openconnect.netflix.com` document the architecture.

## The Decision

Netflix chose to **build its own CDN and physically embed it inside ISPs**, rather than scaling commercial CDN contracts. The parent module README's "origin vs edge vs CDN" trade-off applies: Netflix's catalog is large (multi-petabyte) but highly skewed (the long tail is huge but the head is small), and **predictable nightly prepositioning** beats live cache fills for a workload that is 100 % video-on-demand. They also rejected building edge POPs in commercial datacenters — embedding *inside* the ISP eliminates the most expensive hop (the transit/peering link) and gives sub-10 ms latency to subscribers.

## How It Works

- **Open Connect Appliance (OCA)**: 1U/2U server, ~280 TB of NVMe/SSD storage (later "Storage" models exceed 200 TB), 100 Gbps NICs, FreeBSD + custom Nginx-derived stack.
- **Three deployment tiers**: embedded OCAs inside ISP networks, "site" OCAs at internet exchange points (IXPs), and Netflix's own AWS-backed control plane that schedules content.
- **Prepositioning**: every night during off-peak hours, Netflix pushes the next day's expected popular catalog to each OCA. The cache is **filled by Netflix's central planner**, not by client misses — radically different from a generic CDN.
- **Steering**: the Netflix client app asks the control plane for a sorted list of OCAs; the planner picks the closest healthy OCA with the content, falling back to a peer or origin.
- Reported scale: **17,000+ OCAs in 1,000+ locations across 175+ countries** (publicly described on `openconnect.netflix.com`); **>200 Tbps aggregate serving capacity** at peak.
- **ISP relationship**: free hardware + free shipping + free ongoing replacement. The ISP saves transit cost; Netflix saves CDN cost. Win-win is the explicit pitch.
- Content is **encrypted at rest** on the OCA; keys are delivered to clients separately via the control plane.
- Health: OCAs report telemetry to AWS; unhealthy ones are pulled from steering within minutes.
- HTTP Adaptive Streaming (DASH / HLS-style) with per-title ABR ladders generated by Netflix's encoding pipeline.

## What Surprised Engineers

Netflix's biggest non-obvious lesson is that **predictable workloads beat reactive caches**. Generic CDNs cache on miss and evict on LRU; Open Connect knows tomorrow's hit list because Netflix is the only customer and has its own recommendation logs. This let them run OCAs at ~95 % cache hit rate vs ~70–80 % typical for a general-purpose CDN. The flip side: **a new content launch (Stranger Things season premiere) creates a synchronized global spike** that prepositioning must anticipate by hours; missing the preposition window means the origin and IXP-tier OCAs get hammered.

## Trade-offs in Their Choice

| Win | Cost |
|---|---|
| Most bytes served from inside the user's ISP — single-digit-ms latency, no transit cost | Massive capex on tens of thousands of physical boxes |
| Cache hit rate >95 % vs ~75 % on commercial CDNs | Only useful for *Netflix's* catalog; not a general-purpose CDN play |
| ISP saves money → friendly peering relationship | Field operations are a real, ongoing problem (RMAs, firmware, ISP rack space) |

## Lessons for Your Interview

- When designing a video-streaming system, separate the **catalog** (cold, huge) from the **head** (hot, small) and propose **prepositioning the head into edge caches** during off-peak hours.
- Distinguish a **dedicated CDN** (workload-specific, predictable) from a **generic CDN** (multi-tenant, reactive); interviewers reward this contrast.
- Use **IXP / ISP-embedded edges** as the answer to "how do you reach users in emerging markets" — bandwidth is the bottleneck, latency is secondary.
- Mention **steering = control-plane decision, not DNS** for video: the client asks an API, not a resolver, which gives richer per-title routing.
- Cite cache hit rate as a *first-class* metric in your design, not a side note.
